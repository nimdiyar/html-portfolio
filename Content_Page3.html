DESCRIPTION

The Mauritius Strategy of Implementation (MSI) of 2005 and the MSI+5 Outcome document, in addition to the 1994 Barbados Programme of Action (BPOA), acknowledged that small island developing states (SIDS) have unique vulnerabilities and characteristics despite facing development imperatives and economic hardships common to developing nations. The Future We Want, which was adopted at the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development (also known as Rio+20) in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil in June 2012, highlights the special and unique vulnerabilities of SIDS, including their small size, remoteness, limited resource base and export base, and exposure to external economic shocks and global environmental challenges, including a wide range of impacts from climate change and potentially more frequent

1. Economic and Environmental Vulnerabilities.

SIDS are extremely vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, with many of their lands lying only five meters or less above sea level, making projected sea-level rise a direct threat to their existence. The economic cost associated with sea-level rise is significant, with current estimated annual damages from coastal flooding amounting to €1.54 billion for all SIDS combined . Without adaptation, these damages could increase 9 to 11 times by mid-century. Additionally, SIDS lost USD$ 153 billion due to weather-, climate-, and water-related hazards from 1970 to 2020.








2. Adaptation and Mitigation Efforts.

SIDS leaders have advocated for the most ambitious target under the Paris Agreement, limiting global warming to 1.5°C . They have also established marine protected areas and are participating in global conservation efforts, such as the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration . The Marshall Islands, for example, is developing a 10-year plan to prioritize actions for climate change adaptation .



3.Future Directions.

A new sustainable development blueprint for SIDS will be adopted at the Fourth SIDS Conference hosted by Antigua and Barbuda in 2024. It is crucial to identify effective international mechanisms to share the burden of financing for adaptation and Loss and Damage (L&D) costs among responsible countries. Multilateral Development Banks and innovative financing mechanisms must be further mobilized to build up resilience in SIDS.

In conclusion, SIDS are at the forefront of experiencing the adverse effects of climate change. Despite their low contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions, they bear the brunt of its impacts. International support, innovative adaptation strategies, and strong partnerships are essential for SIDS to navigate the challenges posed by climate change and to achieve sustainable development.
